WHAT IS NATO?
NATO, formed in 1949, is a military alliance of 30 North American and European countries. Centered on collective defense, it pledges mutual support in case of attack. NATO promotes democratic values and engages in crisis management. Decision-making is based on consensus, addressing modern security challenges like terrorism and cyber threats.
WHY WAS NATO INVENTED?
NATO, the North Atlantic Treaty Organization, was established primarily for collective defense and security in the aftermath of World War II. It was founded on April 4, 1949, through the signing of the North Atlantic Treaty by 12 Western countries.

CHINESE ESPIONAGE ON AFRICAN UNION HEADQUARTERS
In January 2017, allegations emerged that the Chinese government spied on the African Union (AU) headquarters in Addis Ababa. Surveillance reportedly began in 2012 during construction. China and Huawei denied the claims. The AU, funded entirely by China at $200 million, replaced Huawei's technology, strengthening encryption. Le Monde Afrique disclosed the events in January 2018. Both the AU and China rejected the allegations, with Chinese officials deeming them absurd. In June 2019, the AU and Huawei signed an agreement for increased IT cooperation. In 2020, Japan's Computer Emergency Response Team reported suspected hacking by a Chinese group named "Bronze President," extracting footage from AU Headquarters' security cameras.

SUSPENSIONS OF AU MEMBERS
In recent years, several African Union member states have experienced suspensions due to political instability, notably marked by military coups and associated violence. Burkina Faso faced suspension in both September 2015 and January 2022, each time following a military coup. Guinea went through two suspensions, the first from December 2008 to January 2011 after a coup, and the second in September 2021. Mali, on the other hand, faced suspensions in March 2012 until October 2013 and again from August 19 to October 8, 2020, due to military coups. The country is currently suspended since June 1, 2021, following another coup. Sudan experienced suspension on June 6, 2019, due to violence following a coup during the 2018â€“19 Sudanese protests. The suspension was lifted three months later. However, another suspension occurred on October 25, 2021, following yet another coup. These instances underscore the challenges some African nations face in achieving political stability, prompting the African Union to grapple with complex dynamics and work towards supporting peaceful transitions and sustainable governance on the continent.

THE PROBLEM WITH WESTERN SAHARA IN THE ARAB LEAGUE
Western Sahara is not a full member of the Arab League. The status of Western Sahara is a complex and disputed issue, and its political status is a matter of contention between different parties. Morocco, which claims sovereignty over Western Sahara, is a member of the Arab League, and its position is generally supported by some Arab countries within the league. However, the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic (SADR), declared by the Polisario Front, represents the Sahrawi people in Western Sahara and has sought international recognition. MINURSO, the United Nations Mission for the Referendum in Western Sahara, was established in 1991 to monitor the ceasefire between Morocco and the Polisario Front and facilitate a self-determination referendum for the people of Western Sahara. However, challenges and delays in conducting the planned referendum have persisted due to the complex political landscape and differing perspectives among involved parties. Despite these obstacles, MINURSO remains a vital component of international efforts, working towards a comprehensive and mutually agreeable resolution to the longstanding conflict in Western Sahara.
SUSPENSION OF SYRIA IN THE ARAB LEAGUE
In 2011, amidst the widespread protests and uprisings of the Arab Spring, Syria faced suspension from the Arab League. The move was a response to the Syrian government's brutal crackdown on civilian demonstrations demanding political reforms and greater freedoms. The Arab League, composed of member states from the Arab world, expressed deep concern over the escalating violence and human rights abuses perpetrated by the Syrian regime. The suspension was a significant diplomatic step, reflecting the regional discontent with the Syrian government's handling of the unrest. The Arab League aimed to pressure Syria to address the grievances of its population and engage in meaningful political reforms. However, the Syrian government, led by President Bashar al-Assad, resisted external interference and continued its military crackdown on dissent.

CRITICISM OF ECOWAS HANDLING COUP REGIMES
The recent ECOWAS summit in the Ghanaian capital on April 29, 2022, brought to light significant criticism regarding the West African bloc's effectiveness in addressing coup regimes. Despite calls for military juntas in Mali, Guinea, and Burkina Faso to promptly return political power to civilians, these appeals were disregarded. The criticism extends to the varying and inconsistently enforced measures and sanctions adopted by ECOWAS, with minimal consequences observed for the coup regimes. The lack of a unified and robust response has sparked calls for urgent reforms and the establishment of supranational bodies with the authority to enforce internationally-binding rules. The handling of crises in Mali, Guinea, and Burkina Faso has underscored the challenges ECOWAS faces in fulfilling its role effectively.
